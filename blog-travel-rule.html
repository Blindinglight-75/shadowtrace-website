Following the Money in a Multi-Chain World: The Reality of Crypto Crime Investigations

Illicit funds routinely move across multiple networks, decentralised exchanges, bridges, and layer-2 systems. Understanding and navigating this multi-chain reality is now a core investigative requirement.

ShadowTrace Research
Published 8 January 2026

Early cryptocurrency investigations often focused on a single blockchain. Today, that assumption no longer holds.

Illicit funds routinely move across multiple networks, decentralised exchanges, bridges, and layer-2 systems. Criminal actors exploit this fragmentation to slow investigations and obscure attribution.

For law enforcement, understanding and navigating this multi-chain reality is now a core investigative requirement.

Why Criminals Move Across Chains

Cross-chain activity is not accidental. It is often deliberate and strategic:

  • Bridges enable rapid movement between ecosystems
  • Decentralised services reduce reliance on identifiable intermediaries
  • Layer-2 networks can complicate transaction visibility
  • Fragmentation increases investigative effort

Each hop adds friction for investigators — unless they have unified visibility.

The Limits of Manual Tracing

Manual transaction tracing across chains presents several risks:

  • Increased chance of missed links
  • Difficulty maintaining consistent documentation
  • Limited ability to revisit or reproduce analysis
  • Significant time investment per case

As transaction volumes grow, manual approaches do not scale.

Unified Visibility as an Investigative Advantage

Modern blockchain intelligence platforms address this challenge by:

  • Aggregating data from multiple supported networks
  • Visualising cross-chain flows within a single investigation
  • Highlighting bridge usage and obfuscation patterns
  • Maintaining a consistent audit trail across networks

This does not eliminate complexity, but it makes it manageable.

Maintaining Evidentiary Integrity

Cross-chain investigations raise additional evidentiary concerns:

  • Can the path of funds be clearly explained?
  • Are assumptions documented?
  • Can another investigator follow the same trail?

Platforms that emphasise reproducibility and documentation help ensure that complexity does not undermine credibility.

Conclusion

Crypto crime is increasingly multi-chain by design. Law enforcement agencies must adapt accordingly.

The ability to follow funds across networks — while preserving clarity and defensibility — is no longer optional. It is foundational to effective modern investigations.
